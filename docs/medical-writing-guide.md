### 1.2 How to Use "Osseointegration" Correctly

**Correct:**
- "During osseointegration — typically 3 to 6 months — the titanium implant bonds with the jawbone."
- "Osseointegration is the biological process by which bone cells grow around the implant surface."

**Incorrect:**
- "Once the implant fuses permanently" — do not use "permanently" without qualification; use "bonds" or "integrates"
- "The implant becomes part of your bone forever" — "forever" is unsubstantiated; osseointegration can fail
- "Instant osseointegration" — osseointegration takes weeks to months; "instant" is factually wrong

### 1.3 Terms Requiring Qualification

These terms are clinically accurate but can mislead patients if used without context. Always add the indicated qualifier.

| Term | Required qualifier | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Permanent | "long-lasting" or specify the expected lifespan | "long-lasting restoration, with implants commonly functioning for 15–25+ years with proper care" |
| Painless | "minimally discomforting" or describe anaesthesia | "performed under local anaesthesia, most patients report minimal discomfort" |
| Natural | specify the aspect (look, feel, or function) | "natural-looking appearance" or "natural chewing function" |
| Same-day | clarify what is same-day (placement vs. final prosthesis) | "same-day provisional restoration; final crown placed after osseointegration" |
| Guaranteed success | never use — see §8 Red Lines | — |

### 1.4 Prohibited Terms

Never use these terms in any context:

- "100% success rate" / "guaranteed results"
- "pain-free" (use "minimally discomforting under local anaesthesia")
- "instant implant" or "instant teeth" without clarifying provisional vs. permanent
- "miracle" in any clinical context
- "cure" for dental conditions (implants restore function; they do not cure underlying disease)
- "the best implant" without evidence-based comparative data
- Superlatives without cited comparative evidence ("the only", "the most advanced in Azerbaijan") unless verifiably true

### 1.5 Trademark and Brand Handling

- **All-on-4®** carries a registered trademark; always include the ® symbol on first mention per page.
- Brand names (Nobel Biocare, Straumann, Osstem, etc.) must be written exactly as the manufacturer specifies — check the brand's own style guide.
- Do not claim equivalence or superiority between brands without published comparative clinical data.

## 2. Medical Accuracy Rules

### 2.1 Fact-Checking Requirements

Every piece of clinical content must pass this checklist before approval:

- [ ] All statistics cite a specific source (journal, year, sample size)
- [ ] Procedure timelines match current clinical consensus (see §5)
- [ ] Risk and contraindication information is present and accurate
- [ ] No claims that contradict the cited source
- [ ] Drug or anaesthesia references align with current protocols
- [ ] Recovery timeframes are ranges, not single fixed values

### 2.2 Evidence Tier Policy

Use the highest available evidence tier for each claim:

| Tier | Source type | Suitable for |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Systematic review / meta-analysis in peer-reviewed journal | Success rate statistics, efficacy claims |
| 2 | RCT or prospective cohort study | Procedure outcomes, comparative claims |
| 3 | Clinical guidelines (ADA, EAO, ITI) | Standard of care statements |
| 4 | Manufacturer IFU / clinical data on file | Material-specific properties |
| 5 | Expert consensus / case series | Technique descriptions where RCT data is sparse |

Do not cite Tier 5 evidence for quantified outcome claims (e.g. "X% success rate"). Use Tier 1–3 only for statistics.

### 2.3 Required Source Format

When citing evidence inline:

> "Studies show implant survival rates exceeding 95% at 10 years (Pjetursson et al., 2012, *Clinical Oral Implants Research*)."

Hyperlink to PubMed or the publisher DOI where possible. If the page cannot carry inline citations, maintain a reference list in the page metadata for editorial review.

### 2.4 Claims That Always Require Evidence

These specific claim categories require a cited source before they can appear in any content:

- Any percentage (success rate, survival rate, patient satisfaction)
- Any specific timeline claim ("heals in X weeks")
- Comparative claims ("better than traditional dentures in X aspect")
- Statements about bone preservation, aesthetics, or chewing function improvement
- Technology performance claims (accuracy of guided surgery, fit of digital workflow)

### 2.5 Self-Audit Checklist for Writers

Before submitting content for medical review, run this check:

1. Can every factual sentence be verified against a cited source?
2. Are all procedure steps in the correct clinical order?
3. Does the content mention risks, not just benefits?
4. Are timelines expressed as ranges ("3–6 months"), not absolutes?
5. Is there a "consult your dental professional" disclaimer where applicable?

## 3. Regulatory Compliance

### 3.1 Azerbaijan Healthcare Advertising Framework

Healthcare advertising in Azerbaijan is governed by:

- **Law of the Republic of Azerbaijan "On Advertising"** (2009, as amended) — prohibits false or misleading claims about medical services
- **Law of the Republic of Azerbaijan "On Health Protection"** — requires that medical information not create unfounded patient expectations
- **Order No. 179 of the Ministry of Health of Azerbaijan Republic** — requires medical service providers to publish accurate descriptions of procedures, qualifications, and pricing

**Key compliance obligations:**

1. All clinical claims must be accurate and not misleading.
2. Pricing references must include VAT and must not omit foreseeable additional costs.
3. Before/after imagery must represent the clinic's own patients with documented informed consent.
4. Credential claims must be verifiable (named doctor, institution, degree — not "our experts").
5. Testimonials must be genuine; fabricated or paid testimonials violate advertising law.

### 3.2 Required Disclaimers

#### Procedure pages
Every procedure page must include:

> "Individual results may vary. Dental implants are medical devices; suitability depends on bone density, general health, and other individual factors. Consult a qualified dental professional for a personalised assessment."

#### FAQ and blog content with clinical information
> "This content is for informational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Please consult a licensed dental professional before making any treatment decisions."

#### Before/After sections
> "Results shown are for illustrative purposes. Individual outcomes depend on patient-specific factors including oral health, bone density, and compliance with aftercare instructions. These images are from our own patients and published with written informed consent."

#### Pricing references
> "Prices shown are approximate. Final treatment cost is determined following a clinical consultation and depends on individual treatment requirements."

### 3.3 Credential and Qualification Standards

- Name the qualified professional responsible for clinical content: "Reviewed by Dr. [Name], [Specialty], [Institution]"
- Do not use generic phrases like "our medical team" without named attribution for clinical claims
- Degrees and certifications must be from recognised institutions; list the accrediting body if not widely known in Azerbaijan

### 3.4 GDPR and Patient Privacy (EU-Adjacent Standard)

Azerbaijan is not subject to GDPR but the clinic serves EU patients and should apply EU-adjacent standards:

- Patient photographs require written informed consent specifying online use
- Store consent forms; link them to the image asset in your CMS
- Do not include identifiable patient information in testimonials without explicit consent

## 4. Patient Communication Tone

### 4.1 Core Tone Principles

| Principle | What it means in practice |
|---|---|
| Empathetic | Acknowledge the anxiety patients feel about dental procedures; validate their concern before moving to clinical information |
| Professional | Use correct clinical terms; explain them in plain language — never talk down to the patient |
| Honest | Describe realistic timelines, recovery, and outcomes; do not only lead with best-case scenarios |
| Non-alarming | Present risks matter-of-factly, not dramatically; contextualise with low statistical frequency where data supports it |
| Empowering | Help patients understand what questions to ask and what to expect; give them agency |

### 4.2 Pain and Recovery Language

**Do:**
- "Most patients experience mild discomfort and swelling for 2–3 days following surgery, which typically resolves with prescribed pain relief."
- "Local anaesthesia is used throughout the procedure; patients generally report the experience as less uncomfortable than expected."
- "Recovery is gradual. Soft foods are recommended for the first week; most patients return to normal activities within 1–2 days."

**Do not:**
- "The procedure is completely painless." (factually wrong; there is post-operative discomfort)
- "You'll be back to normal the same day." (misleading; soft diet restrictions apply)
- "Many patients find the surgery terrifying at first." (alarmist framing)

### 4.3 Readability Standards

- Target reading level: Flesch-Kincaid Grade 8–10 for Azerbaijani and Russian versions
- Maximum sentence length: 25 words for patient-facing copy
- Avoid jargon without explanation; every clinical term should be followed by a plain-language equivalent in parentheses on first use, e.g. "osseointegration (the process by which bone grows around the implant)"
- Use active voice wherever possible
- Use second person ("you", "your dentist") for patient-facing content

### 4.4 Sensitive Topics Framing

**Tooth loss and aesthetics:** Do not use shaming language ("unsightly gaps", "embarrassing smile"). Use neutral clinical framing: "missing teeth can affect chewing function, speech, and long-term bone health."

**Financial anxiety:** Acknowledge cost concerns directly and link to financing options; do not minimise cost concerns or claim implants are "affordable for everyone."

**Failed previous treatments:** When the content targets patients who've had previous implant failures, adopt a solutions-oriented tone without disparaging prior providers.

## 5. Procedure Descriptions

Standard write-ups for each core service. Writers must use these as the baseline; deviations require medical review sign-off.

### 5.1 Single Dental Implant Placement

**What it is:** Placement of a titanium implant fixture into the jawbone to replace the root of a missing tooth, followed by attachment of a crown once osseointegration is complete.

**Typical timeline:**
- Consultation and CBCT imaging: 1 visit
- Implant placement surgery: 45–90 minutes under local anaesthesia
- Osseointegration: 3–6 months (longer with bone grafting)
- Abutment and crown placement: 1–2 visits after osseointegration confirmed
- Total treatment: 4–9 months

**Key patient points:**
- Performed under local anaesthesia; sedation available on request
- Minor swelling and discomfort for 2–5 days post-surgery
- Soft diet for 1–2 weeks following placement
- Implant survival rates exceed 95% at 10 years in healthy patients (Pjetursson et al., 2012)

**Contraindications to mention:**
- Uncontrolled diabetes, active periodontal disease, smoking (significantly increases failure risk), insufficient bone volume (may be resolved with grafting), certain medications (bisphosphonates, immunosuppressants — require specialist assessment), active oral infections

### 5.2 Bone Grafting (Augmentation)

**What it is:** A procedure to increase bone volume in the jaw where bone has been lost, creating the foundation needed for implant placement.

**Types:**
- Autograft (patient's own bone — highest biological compatibility)
- Allograft (donor bone — processed and sterilised)
- Xenograft (bovine-derived — widely used, well-documented)
- Alloplast (synthetic — e.g. hydroxyapatite)

**Typical timeline:**
- Grafting procedure: 30–90 minutes
- Healing and graft integration: 4–9 months before implant placement
- Total added to treatment: 4–9 months

**Key patient points:**
- May be performed at same visit as extraction or as a separate procedure
- Some swelling and discomfort for 3–7 days
- Diet restrictions during healing

**Do not state:** that bone grafts "always work" or guarantee sufficient volume for implants. Graft outcomes depend on patient factors.

### 5.3 Sinus Lift (Maxillary Sinus Augmentation)

**What it is:** A surgical procedure to add bone to the upper jaw (maxilla) in the area of the molars and premolars, where the sinus cavity has expanded following tooth loss.

**Approaches:**
- Lateral window technique (for larger volume requirements)
- Transcrestal (osteotome) technique (minimally invasive, for smaller lifts)

**Typical timeline:**
- Sinus lift procedure: 1–2 hours under local anaesthesia
- Healing before implant placement: 4–9 months (lateral) or may be simultaneous with transcrestal if bone height ≥5 mm
- Total addition to treatment: 0–9 months depending on technique

**Key patient points:**
- Risk of sinus membrane perforation (managed intraoperatively; does not prevent implant if repaired)
- Post-op: avoid blowing nose, sneezing with mouth open for 2 weeks
- Antibiotics and decongestants typically prescribed

### 5.4 Computer-Guided Implant Surgery

**What it is:** Use of a 3D surgical guide derived from CBCT imaging and digital planning software to precisely position the implant during surgery.

**Benefits to describe (evidence-based):**
- Improved accuracy of implant positioning versus freehand surgery (Van Assche et al., 2012)
- Reduced risk of damage to adjacent anatomical structures
- Potential for flapless (minimally invasive) surgery in suitable cases
- Pre-surgical visualisation of the final result

**What not to claim:**
- Do not state guided surgery "eliminates all risk" — accuracy improves, it does not achieve perfection
- Do not claim guided surgery is always faster — planning phase adds time; total treatment time is comparable

**Typical description:** "Using CBCT scan data and 3Shape (or equivalent) planning software, a custom surgical guide is fabricated. During surgery, the guide positions the drill and implant at the planned angle and depth, improving precision and reducing soft tissue trauma."

### 5.5 All-on-4® Protocol

**What it is:** A full-arch implant restoration using four strategically placed implants to support a fixed prosthesis (full bridge), restoring an entire dental arch.

**Key clinical details:**
- Two anterior implants placed vertically; two posterior implants angled up to 45° to maximise bone contact and avoid anatomical structures
- Provisional prosthesis placed on the day of surgery in suitable patients
- Final prosthesis placed after osseointegration (typically 3–6 months)
- Reduces or eliminates need for bone grafting in many cases

**Accurate claims:**
- "All-on-4® is designed to provide a fixed, non-removable full-arch restoration supported by four implants."
- "The angled posterior implants make use of available bone, reducing the need for bone grafting in many cases."
- Cite: Malo et al., 2011 (*Clinical Implant Dentistry and Related Research*) for long-term data

**Claims to avoid:**
- "All-on-4® works for everyone" — not all patients are candidates; bone volume and health assessment required
- "Same-day teeth" without clarifying these are provisional — the final restoration requires osseointegration

### 5.6 All-on-6

**What it is:** Full-arch restoration using six implants, providing additional support and load distribution compared to All-on-4®.

**Key points:** Follow the same accuracy and disclaimer standards as All-on-4®. Six-implant protocols are particularly indicated where additional posterior support is required or All-on-4® geometry is not anatomically optimal.

### 5.7 Crown Placement

**What it is:** Attachment of the final porcelain, zirconia, or ceramic crown to the abutment after confirmed osseointegration.

**Accurate claims:**
- "Zirconia crowns offer excellent aesthetics and strength, with a natural translucency that closely resembles natural enamel."
- "The crown is custom-fabricated from impressions or a digital scan to match the shade and shape of surrounding teeth."

**Do not claim:** Specific lifespan guarantees for the crown (material and occlusal load affect longevity); use "crowns typically last 10–15+ years with good oral hygiene" based on clinical data.

## 6. Content Structure Templates

### 6.1 Procedure Page Template

```
[H1] [Procedure Name] in Baku | Digitalimplant.az

[Lead paragraph — 2–3 sentences]
What the procedure is, who it benefits, and the key patient-facing outcome (not a medical claim — a plain-language summary).

[H2] What is [Procedure Name]?
Clinical explanation in patient-friendly language. Include one correctly sourced statistic if available.

[H2] Who is a candidate?
Indications and ideal patient profile. Must include at least one contraindication note.

[H2] The Procedure: Step by Step
Numbered list of the main steps. Use present tense, second person.

[H2] Recovery and Aftercare
Timeline in ranges. Soft diet, activity restrictions, signs to watch for. Must include "Contact us if you experience X" guidance.

[H2] Frequently Asked Questions
3–5 Q&A pairs. Follow FAQ answer format (§6.2).

[Disclaimer block — see §3.2]

[CTA] Book a consultation / Request more information
```

### 6.2 FAQ Answer Format

Each FAQ answer must:
1. Directly answer the question in the first sentence
2. Add qualifying context (e.g. "this varies by individual")
3. Suggest a next step if appropriate ("Your dentist can assess…")
4. Stay under 80 words for clarity

**Example:**

> **Q: How long do dental implants last?**
>
> Dental implants can last 15–25 years or more with proper care and good oral hygiene. Their longevity depends on bone health, oral hygiene habits, and regular dental check-ups. Lifestyle factors such as smoking significantly reduce implant lifespan. Your dentist will discuss what to expect based on your individual circumstances.

### 6.3 Blog Article Structure for Medical Topics

```
[H1] [SEO-optimised title that accurately reflects the content]

Author byline: Written by [Name] | Medically reviewed by Dr. [Name], [Credential]
Date published / last updated

[Introduction — 100–150 words]
Hook, why the topic matters to the reader, and what the article covers. No medical claims without sources.

[H2] [Subtopic 1]
[H2] [Subtopic 2]
...

[H2] When to See a Dental Professional
Specific scenarios where the reader should seek advice — always include for health topics.

[H2] Key Takeaways
Bullet list of 3–5 evidence-based points from the article.

[Informational disclaimer — see §3.2]

[References section]
Numbered citation list in Vancouver or APA format.

[CTA] — relevant to the article's topic
```

## 7. Before/After Content Rules

### 7.1 Legal Requirements

1. **Informed consent is mandatory.** Every patient whose photograph appears on the site must sign a written consent form that specifically authorises:
   - Use of their photograph on the Digitalimplant.az website
   - Use in promotional materials (if applicable)
   - The form must be dated and stored with patient records

2. **No stock photos for before/after.** All before/after imagery must be actual patients treated at Digitalimplant.az. Using third-party imagery as your own clinical results is deceptive advertising and violates Azerbaijan advertising law.

3. **Authenticity.** Images must not be digitally altered to enhance results beyond colour correction and cropping. Teeth whitening, added brightness, or any modification that makes results appear better than they are is prohibited.

### 7.2 Mandatory Caption Requirements

Every before/after image pair must include:

- Indication of what procedure was performed
- Timeframe ("after 12 months")
- The disclaimer: "Result shown is for one patient. Individual outcomes vary."
- Patient initials or pseudonym if identified (never full name without explicit consent for name use)

### 7.3 Representative Selection

Do not cherry-pick only exceptional cases. Where the site shows multiple before/after examples, they should represent a realistic distribution of outcomes, including typical (not just exceptional) results.

### 7.4 Prohibited Before/After Practices

- Showing results from other clinics or implant providers as your own
- Before/after comparisons that include natural teeth vs. implant crowns without clearly labelling the natural state
- Using animated or 3D renders presented as real patient photos
- Showing before photos that are deliberately unflattering (poor lighting, open mouth grimacing) next to idealised after photos

## 8. Red Lines

These items must **never** appear on Digitalimplant.az in any language or format. No exceptions without CEO sign-off and legal review.

### 8.1 Outcome Guarantees

- ❌ "We guarantee your implants will last a lifetime."
- ❌ "100% success rate."
- ❌ "Your implants will never fail."
- ❌ Any money-back guarantee tied to clinical outcomes (this constitutes a medical guarantee, which is prohibited in Azerbaijan and ethically problematic)

**Why:** Implant success depends on patient biology, compliance, and systemic health factors outside the clinic's control. Outcome guarantees create false expectations and legal liability.

### 8.2 Specific Outcome Promises

- ❌ "You will look 20 years younger."
- ❌ "You'll be completely pain-free immediately after surgery."
- ❌ "No one will know you have implants."
- ❌ "This will solve your dental problems forever."

### 8.3 Unproven or Pseudoscientific Claims

- ❌ References to "miracle" or "revolutionary" procedures without published clinical validation
- ❌ Claims about stem cells, regenerative techniques, or nanotechnology unless specifically validated for the product in use with peer-reviewed evidence
- ❌ Implying that the clinic's protocol is medically superior to standard-of-care without comparative clinical data

### 8.4 Inappropriate Comparisons

- ❌ "Unlike other clinics in Baku, we use only genuine implants." (implies others use counterfeit; substantiate or remove)
- ❌ Naming competitor clinics in any negative comparative context
- ❌ Claiming "best results in Azerbaijan" without independently verified data

### 8.5 Contraindication Suppression

- ❌ Claiming implants are suitable "for everyone" or "regardless of bone density"
- ❌ Describing bone grafting as "always successful" or "guaranteed to work"
- ❌ Omitting the smoking contraindication from implant suitability content

### 8.6 Unverified Credentials

- ❌ Listing training programmes, certifications, or memberships that are not verifiable
- ❌ "World-class" or "internationally recognised" without named verifiable body
- ❌ Publication claims without DOI or citation

### 9.1 Content Production Workflow

```
Writer drafts content
        ↓

        ↓
SEO Manager review (keywords, structure)
        ↓

        ↓

        ↓

        ↓
```

### 9.2 Medical Review Checklist

The Medical Content Manager applies this checklist to every piece submitted for review:

**Accuracy**
- [ ] All statistics are cited with source, year, and study type
- [ ] Timelines are ranges (not fixed values) and match current clinical consensus
- [ ] Procedure descriptions follow the standard write-ups in §5
- [ ] Contraindications are present and accurate

**Compliance**
- [ ] Required disclaimers are present (§3.2)
- [ ] No prohibited terms used (§1.4)
- [ ] No red-line content (§8)
- [ ] Before/after rules followed if imagery included (§7)

**Tone**
- [ ] Empathetic but professional tone (§4)
- [ ] Pain/recovery described honestly without alarming language (§4.2)
- [ ] Active voice, plain language, sentence length ≤25 words

**Attribution**
- [ ] Named medical reviewer credited
- [ ] Sources available for all cited claims

### 9.3 Turnaround Times

| Content type | Medical review SLA |
|---|---|
| New procedure page | 3 business days |
| Updated procedure page | 2 business days |
| FAQ addition (≤5 questions) | 1 business day |
| Blog article with clinical claims | 2 business days |
| Translation medical spot-check | 1 business day |

### 9.4 Flagging High-Risk Content

Content must be escalated to the CEO before publishing if it:
- Makes a novel claim not supported by the §5 standard write-ups
- Names a specific product as superior without head-to-head comparative data
- Contains any pricing or outcome commitment
- Includes any testimonial about a specific patient outcome (to verify consent)
- Involves any claim about competitor clinics

## 10. Multilingual Medical Writing

### 10.1 Azerbaijani as the Master Language

All original content is written in Azerbaijani (az_AZ). Russian is the secondary language. Translation flows AZ → RU; do not write RU content directly without an AZ original.

### 10.2 Terminology Precision in Azerbaijani

Azerbaijani medical terminology for dental implants is still evolving and borrows heavily from English and Russian. Follow these rules:

1. **Use the approved Azerbaijani term** from §1.1 wherever one exists.
2. **On first use per page**, add the English term in parentheses: e.g. "osteointegrasiya (osseointegration)".
3. **For terms with no established Azerbaijani equivalent**, use the English term and provide a plain-language Azerbaijani explanation.
4. **Do not calque from Russian** — derive Azerbaijani terms from Turkish or English sources to avoid Soviet-era medical terminology that may be unfamiliar to younger patients.

### 10.3 Russian Medical Terminology Standards

Russian dental implant terminology is well-established. Use standard clinical Russian:

- имплантат (implant body/fixture) — preferred over implant as a borrowed word
- остеоинтеграция — do not write "osteointegration" in Cyrillic transliteration
- протезирование — prosthetic treatment (broader context)
- синус-лифт — accepted loanword in Russian dental literature
- компьютерная томография (КТ/КБКТ) — CBCT

Avoid colloquial Russian that may read as insufficiently professional in a clinical context.

### 10.4 Structural Adaptation Rules (AZ → RU)

**What must stay identical:**
- All statistics and their source citations
- All disclaimers (§3.2) — translate word-for-word; do not soften
- All contraindications — do not omit or downgrade severity
- All procedure timelines

**What can be culturally adapted:**
- Tone: Russian-language medical content for this audience can be slightly more formal than the Azerbaijani version; this is acceptable
- Examples and analogies: localise if a cultural reference doesn't translate well
- SEO terms: keyword research is separate (consult SEO Manager) — do not add or remove clinical claims to chase Russian keywords

**What cannot be added in translation:**
- Claims that do not appear in the AZ original
- Softened disclaimers
- Different outcome percentages

### 10.5 Back-Translation Check

For any new procedure page or high-stakes content:
1. Translator completes RU version
2. A second reviewer (not the original translator) reads the RU version independently and flags any divergence from the AZ source
3. Medical Content Manager resolves discrepancies
4. Only the reconciled version proceeds to publish

### 10.6 Language-Specific Red Lines

In addition to the general red lines (§8), these terms are specifically prohibited in the respective languages:

**Azerbaijani:** Avoid "möcüzə" (miracle) in clinical context; avoid "mütləq" (absolute/certain) for any outcome claim.

**Russian:** Avoid "гарантировано" (guaranteed) for any clinical outcome; avoid "навсегда" (forever) for implant longevity; avoid "лучшая клиника" (best clinic) without verifiable supporting evidence.
